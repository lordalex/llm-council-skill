The recommendationA real answer — not "it depends"

Go with the Executor's sequencing, not the Strategist's leap. You don't burn $95k of guaranteed income and a partner's goodwill on an assumption you could falsify in two weeks for free. Spend up to two bounded weeks on the cheap, reversible moves — kill the support backlog (FAQ, canned responses, help widget), run a price test, pull the actual churn number — then walk into a part-time negotiation with real numbers. If part-time is refused, quit-now becomes the live option — not before. And if two weeks in you're not recovering at all, that is itself the answer: the burnout clock is shorter than the diagnostic clock, and you negotiate reduced hours regardless of what the business data says.

The Contrarian Don't quit — the growth math extrapolates one viral spike; try a $500/month fix first. sonnet

The math is the flaw everyone will skip: “$8k/month in 14 months” assumes 10% month-over-month compounds cleanly with zero deceleration. It won't. You had one viral spike five months ago and you're extrapolating a smooth exponential off the tail of it — nobody grows 10% MoM for 14 straight months off a single unpaid channel with no CAC engine. Halve the growth assumption and “keep grinding” is 24+ months away: the option that looks safest is the trap.

Second: this isn't a 3-option decision. The constraint is operational capacity, not product-market fit — support backlog and slow shipping. Quitting solves capacity, but so might templated support responses and a part-time VA for $500/month. Nobody asked “what if I bought back 5 hours/week for $500 instead of $95k/year of income risk?” Try that before any option on the table.

Third: “partner supports the idea” is doing a lot of unexamined work. Supporting a Tuesday-night conversation and covering rent solo in month 6 are different things — has that been stress-tested with numbers, or is it optimism laundered through goodwill?

The First-Principles Thinker Wrong question — buy time without resigning, and define what success even means. sonnet

The real question isn't “quit, negotiate, or grind” — it's “why does this decision require quitting a job at all?” That framing assumes the app needs a full person and the job is the only movable variable. Neither is obviously true.

The constraint is time — not talent, not market. You have paying customers and organic virality, which most founders never get. And there are more ways to buy time than resigning: $2k/month could fund a support contractor, freeing your 10 hours for growth work. Have you actually asked for a 4-day week, or assumed rejection?

Interrogate the framing too: it's not $95k of security vs $24k of income — it's your only asset (time) allocated between a flat wage claim and a claim compounding 10%/month with real optionality. The 8-month runway figure is a red herring dressed as rigor: it assumes revenue stays flat if you quit, contradicting the very premise of quitting.

The unasked question that determines everything: does “success” mean lifestyle income, or building something big? It's missing from the brief entirely.

The Strategist Quit now — only full-time bends the curve. Hard checkpoint at month 6. sonnet

The two numbers sit on different curves — the job's $95k is flat, the same in month 13 as month 1; the app's $2k compounds with intervention. That asymmetry is the whole decision.

But the current growth runs on word-of-mouth off one viral post, tended in 10 distracted hours a week. Word-of-mouth decays as the audience saturates — and an unaddressed support backlog is exactly how you kill it, because churned customers don't refer. Full-time isn't “more hours on the same trajectory”; it's the only path that converts this into a step-function: shipped backlog, cleared queue, a second growth channel actually built. Nights-and-weekends can maintain a curve; it can't bend one.

The part-time option is the trap that looks safest: the company treats you as flight-risk while you still lack the hours to fix the real bottleneck. Given 8 months of runway and a partner backstop: quit now, with a hard month-6 checkpoint — if MRR isn't materially ahead of trajectory, the curve was fake. Corporate optionality barely decays in 6–12 months; the product's window does.

The Executor Two-week diagnostic sprint, then negotiate part-time with numbers in hand. sonnet

Don't quit. Don't negotiate part-time yet either — you don't have the ammunition for that conversation. And option (c) as written is also wrong: growth is limited by your 10 hours/week and you're burned out; grinding harder delays the crash.

Monday morning: buy time by cutting cost, not by quitting income. Over two weeks: (1) kill the support backlog — FAQ docs, canned responses, a help widget; it's probably eating half your 10 hours and it's the most delegable part of the business; (2) price-test +20–30% on new signups — 900 word-of-mouth payers means pricing room; (3) get the one number you don't have: churn. Growth minus churn tells you whether 14 months is real or fantasy.

Then negotiate part-time — with numbers. “I want to go 4 days” is a weak ask. “I run a business with $24k ARR growing 10%/month; I want Fridays; here's the exact scope I'll still cover” is one your manager can actually approve. If they say no — that's when quit-now becomes the live option. Not before. Quitting now is the wrong order of operations, not necessarily the wrong ending.

The Domain SpecialistOpportunity-Cost & Values Part-time first — this is a burnout decision, not a conviction decision. sonnet

Option (c) is internally contradictory: you cannot grind nights to salary replacement when nights are the stated bottleneck. That's not a plan — it's a hope dressed as a spreadsheet.

Opportunity cost, honestly priced: this isn't $95k traded for $24k. It's $95k plus benefits plus career-ladder compounding traded for ~30 hours/week of attention on an asset with real pull. A rational bet — IF those hours plausibly move growth. If the bottleneck is demand, not hours, quitting just burns runway on a business that grows 10% anyway.

Values check: he calls the job “uninspiring” and says he's burned out. Notice what's absent — any statement of loving the app, the customers, or the mission. The stated motivation is escape plus exhaustion, not pull-toward-the-work. That's a burnout decision, not a conviction decision — and burnout decisions made by quitting everything tend to relocate the burnout, not resolve it.

And the partner sentence needs interrogating: find her actual ceiling — six months of covering rent, or resentment by month nine? — now, not when the bank account forces the conversation. Verdict: negotiate part-time first; it's the only option that yields data before spending savings and goodwill.
