Nobody has ever grown 10% MoM for 14 straight months off a single unpaid channel with no CAC engine. Cut that growth assumption in half and the "keep grinding" plan is now 24+ months away, which means option (c) — "just grind a bit longer" — is the one most likely to be a trap disguised as the safe choice.

Second flaw: this isn't actually a 3-option decision. You've diagnosed your own problem — growth is "visibly limited by 10 hours/week" — support backlog and slow shipping. The constraint is operational capacity, not product-market fit. Quitting solves the capacity problem. ### The First-Principles Thinker (`sonnet`)
The real question isn't "quit, negotiate, or grind" — it's "why does this decision require quitting a job at all?" That framing smuggles in an assumption: that the app needs a full person and the job is the only variable you can move. Neither is obviously true.

Look at what's actually constrained: 10 hours/week is the bottleneck — not talent, not market, not product-market fit. You have paying customers and organic virality, which most founders never get. The scarce resource is time, and there are more ways to buy time than resigning. Could $2k/month fund a part-time contractor for support tickets, freeing your 10 hours for the growth-limiting work instead of the maintenance-limiting work? Also interrogate the $95k vs $24k framing — it's not really $95k of security weighed against $24k of income. It's your only asset (time) allocated between a claim that pays a fixed wage regardless of effort, and a claim compounding at 10%/month with optionality to become worth vastly more. The 8-month runway number is a red herring dressed as rigor — it assumes the app's revenue stays flat if you quit, when the entire premise of quitting is that revenue accelerates.

### The Strategist (`sonnet`)

Word-of-mouth growth curves decay hard once the initial audience saturates, and support backlog left unaddressed is exactly how you kill word-of-mouth — churned customers don't refer anyone. The 14-months-to-$8k projection assumes linear continuation of a regime (viral tailwind, novelty) that's already fading. Nights-and-weekends can maintain a curve; it can't bend one.

Second-order cost of "keep grinding": burnout doesn't announce itself as burnout — it announces itself as mediocre performance reviews and a mediocre product. You lose both, slowly.
